    Section 2.2 Linear Functions

    There are many ways to write the equation of a line. For instance, the equations \(y = 3x - 2\text{,}\) \(3x-y = 2\text{,}\) and \(y-1 = 3(x-1)\) all describe the linear function graphed below.
    🔗
    We’ll focus on two often-used conventions for how to write the equation of a line: Point-Slope Form and Slope-Intercept Form.
    🔗

    Subsection 2.2.1 Point-Slope Form

    This form is good if you just want to write the equation of a line and don’t mind if it’s messy. Here are the steps for writing the equation of a line in point-slope form.
    🔗
    1. Find one point on the line, \((a,b)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Find the slope of the line, \(m\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Plug the point and slope into the equation
      \begin{equation*} y-b = m(x-a) \end{equation*}
      In this equation, \(a\text{,}\)\(b\text{,}\) and \(m\) will be numbers that you plug in, and \(x\) and \(y\) will remain as variables.
      🔗
      🔗

    Example 2.2.2. Equation in Point-Slope Form From Graph.

    Find the equation for the given line. Write your answer in point-slope form.
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. First we need to choose a point on the line. I will choose the point \((a,b)=(2,1)\text{,}\) but you could use other points on the graph instead, like \((3,4)\) or \((1,-2)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Using two points on the graph, we need to find the slope. I will use the points \((2,1)\) and \((3,4)\text{.}\) With these, I compute the slope.
      \begin{equation*} m = \frac{1-4}{2-3} = \frac{-3}{-1} = 3 \end{equation*}
      You may use different points from the graph to compute the slope, but no matter which points you choose, you should get \(m=3\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Plugging this point and slope into the formula for Point-Slope Form, we get
      \begin{equation*} y-1 = 3(x-2) \end{equation*}
      If you choose a different point in Step 1, it will result in a different but equivalent answer. For example, if you use the point \((3,4)\text{,}\) your answer will be
      \begin{equation*} y-4 = 3(x-3)\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.2.4. Equation in Point-Slope Form From Data.

    Find the equation, in point-slope form, of the line that goes through the point \((-2, -4)\) and has a slope of \(\frac{1}{4}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    In this example, we are given the point \((a,b) = (-2, -4)\) and the slope \(m = \frac{1}{4}\text{.}\) We plug them into the formula for Point-Slope Form, we get
    \begin{equation*} y - (-4) = \frac{1}{4}(x - (-2)) \end{equation*}
    which simplifies to become
    \begin{equation*} y+4 = \frac{1}{4}(x+2) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.2.5. You Try It!

    Find the equation for the given line. Write your answer in point-slope form.
    🔗
    Answer.
    If you use the point \((-2,1)\text{,}\) the answer is \(y-1 = \frac{4}{5}(x+2)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If you use the point \((3,5)\text{,}\) the answer is \(y-5 = \frac{4}{5}(x-3)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.2.2 Slope-Intercept Form

    If you want the equation of a line to match function notation (\(y\) or \(f(x)\) on one side by itself), then you should use Slope-Intercept Form to write the equation of your line. As a bonus, as the name suggests, this form encodes important geometric information about the graph of the line, namely its slope and \(y\)-intercept.
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.7.

    The slope-intercept form of the equation of the line with slope \(m\) and \(y\)-intercept \(b\) is
    \begin{equation*} y = mx+b \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Recall that the \(y\)-intercept of a graph describe where the graph crosses the \(y\)-axis. So when we say that the \(y\)-intercept of a graph is \(b\text{,}\) that means the graph crosses the \(y\)-axis at the point \((0,b)\text{.}\) A linear function will always have a unique \(y\)-intercept. Can you explain why? If not, review the Vertical Line Test.
    🔗
    Because the slope and \(y\)-intercept of a line are unique, the slope-intercept form of the equation for a line has the added benefit that it is unique, too. That is, there is only one way to write the slope-intercept form of the equation for a given line, so in our examples and exercises, we will all get the same answers.
    🔗
    The steps for writing the equation of a line in slope-intercept form based on its graph will depend on whether or not the \(y\)-intercept, \(b\text{,}\) is clear from the graph.
    🔗
    If you can tell the \(y\)-intercept by looking at the graph:
    🔗
    1. Find the \(y\)-intercept, \(b\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Use two points on the graph to calculate the slope, \(m\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Plug \(b\) and \(m\) into the equation
      \begin{equation*} y = mx+b. \end{equation*}
      In this equation, \(m\) and \(b\) will be numbers that you plug in and \(x\) and \(y\) will remain as variables.
      🔗
      🔗
    If you can’t tell the exact value of the \(y\)-intercept by looking at the graph:
    🔗
    1. Use two points on the graph to calculate the slope, \(m\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Choose one point from the graph, \((a,b)\text{,}\) and plug it into Point-Slope Form:
      \begin{equation*} y-b = m(x-a) \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Solve the equation for \(y\) and simplify so that it is written in the form \(y = mx+b\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗

    Example 2.2.8. Equation in Slope-Intercept Form From Graph.

    Find the equation for the given line. Write your answer in slope-intercept form.
    🔗
    Solution.
    From this graph, we can see that the line crosses the \(y\)-axis at the point \((0,2)\text{,}\) so the \(y\)- intercept is \(b=2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To find the slope, choose two points from the graph. For instance, we can use the points \((0,2)\) and \((3,0)\text{.}\) Then the slope is
    \begin{equation*} m = \frac{0-2}{3-0} = -\frac{2}{3} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Plugging the slope and intercept into the formula \(y = mx+b\text{,}\) our answer is
    \begin{equation*} y = -\frac{2}{3}x+2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.2.10. Equation in Slope-Intercept Form From Graph.

    Find the equation for the given line. Write your answer in point-slope form.
    🔗
    Solution.
    For this graph, it’s not clear what the exact value of the \(y\)-intercept is. I can tell it is between 0 and \(0.5\text{,}\) maybe \(\frac{1}{3}\) or \(0.3\text{.}\) We will need to use the second method to write the equation in slope-intercept form.
    🔗
    Let’s start by finding the slope. For this, we need two points on the line. We can use \((-1,0)\) and \((2,1)\text{.}\) The slope is
    \begin{equation*} m = \frac{1-0}{2-(-1)} = \frac{1}{3} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Choose one of these points, say \((-1,0)\) to write the equation in point-slope form:
    \begin{equation*} y - 0 = \frac{1}{3}(x+1) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Simplifying, we get our answer:
    \begin{equation*} y = \frac{1}{3}x+\frac{1}{3} \end{equation*}
    From this, we can see that the \(y\)-intercept is \(b = \frac{1}{3}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.2.12. Equation in Slope-Intercept Form From Data.

    Find the equation, in slope-intercept form, of the line that goes through the point \((2, 7)\) and has a slope of \(m = \frac{1}{2}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    To get an answer, we will plug the information given into point-slope form, rearrange to get \(y\) by itself, and simplify.
    \begin{align*} y-7 \amp = \frac{1}{2}(x-2) \\ y-7 \amp = \frac{1}{2}x - 1 \\ y \amp = \frac{1}{2}x + 6 \end{align*}
    So our final answer is \(y = \frac{1}{2}x+6\text{.}\)
